Valuation of Down-and-out Put Option

This project aims to derive reasonable no-arbitrage prices for a set of simple derivatives written on an asset exhibiting "non-standard" dynamics. In Section 1, the report focuses on the theoretical analysis of a given stochastic differential equation (SDE) and the empirical assessment of the underlying time series characteristics, primarily by evaluating Gaussian behavior. Subsequently, the optimal set of parameters fitting the empirical data is obtained through maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), utilizing the Euler discretization scheme of the SDE. Sections 2 and 3 are dedicated to deriving inception no-arbitrage prices for three different types of derivative contracts based on the underlying asset: a European put option (priced using three numerical approaches: Monte Carlo simulation, lattice discretization, and a PDE scheme), the American version of the European put option, and a Down-and-Out barrier variant of the same put option.

Impact of valution adjustments on option prices

Developed a simulation-based model to measure counterparty credit, funding, and capital impacts on pricing a down-and-in put option. Simulated interest rates using a calibrated Hull–White short-rate model and equity prices under geometric Brownian motion, with sotchastic rates. Generated exposure profiles through pathwise revaluation of the swap and option, showing a negative impact of valuation adjustments on the option’s value.

Bachelor Thesis: Volatility Forecasting

Assessed the performance of different model classes for forecasting weekly realised volatility of the Financial Times Stock Exchange One Hundred index using daily price data from Refinitiv Workspace over the period from 2004 to 2024. Selected realised volatility as an observable and model-independent measure rather than option-implied volatility. Aligned realised and conditional volatility measures to allow a consistent comparison across modelling approaches. Found that simple autoregressive models provided the most accurate forecasts overall, while neural-network-based methods achieved lower forecasting errors than models based on conditional variance alone, with additional improvements when the two approaches were combined.
